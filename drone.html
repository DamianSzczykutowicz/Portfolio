// 01 — Overview

The goal

The objective was to build a low-cost autonomous delivery platform: a drone that could take off, fly to a user-defined GPS waypoint, and accurately drop a small payload — all on a tight student budget. The challenge was hitting reliable autonomous behavior and a clean mechanical design without expensive off-the-shelf hardware.

I owned the structural design and system integration — starting from an off-the-shelf frame to prove out the concept, then redesigning and 3D printing the entire airframe myself, machining the drop mechanism from aluminum, and wiring up the STM32 flight controller running ArduPilot.

// 02 — Iteration V1

Proving the electronics

The first build ran on an off-the-shelf frame so I could get the core flight stack working before committing to a custom structure — flight controller, GPS module, power distribution, and motors. V1 proved out the autonomy logic and let me debug wiring and signal issues in a controlled setting.

// 03 — Iteration V2

The flying build

For V2 I moved off the stock frame entirely and 3D printed the full airframe in-house. The drop mechanism was CNC-machined from aluminum for strength and a clean, repeatable release, mounted underneath and wired to a dedicated channel on the STM32 flight controller for autonomous triggering under ArduPilot.

// 04 — Mechanical Design

Built from scratch

Rather than relying on the stock frame, I 3D printed the entire airframe in-house. The landing legs went through several iterations in SolidWorks, using topology optimization to cut weight while keeping enough stiffness to survive repeated landings — the lattice cutouts remove material where the FEA showed low stress.

The drop mechanism was CNC-machined from aluminum for durability and a precise, repeatable release — the one part that needed metal-grade strength and tight tolerances.
